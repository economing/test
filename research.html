A Hard Nut to Crack: Identifying Factors Relevant to Chestnut Consumption (written with Erin Lizotte and Trey Malone, Journal of Food Distribution Research 50.2, 2019).
Chestnuts are popular worldwide, but they are not commonly purchased in the United States. Using a survey of over 1,000 U.S. and over 1,000 Chinese consumers, we use geospatial techniques and explore why over half of U.S. consumers have never eaten a chestnut. We test questions regarding key geographic, social, and cultural characteristics of likely U.S. chestnut consumers. Results suggest that immigration patterns weakly affect chestnut consumption but that age is a more important predictor of consumption frequency. Our empirical analysis suggests that consumers in coastal states consume the most chestnuts and that socioeconomic characteristics significantly influence consumption.
Relative Income and Subjective Economic Status: Empirical Evidence from the Perspective of the Collectivism Culture (written in Chinese with Tao Li, Lin Fu and Xingye Jin, Economic Research Journal 12, 2019).
Subjective economic status, the subjective perception of one's economic status in society, depends on not only the objective economic status but also the way people perceive their relative standing and therefore corresponds to the cultural context. We study the impact of the objective relative income on subjective economic status using data from the China Family Panel Studies (CFPS) and the regression results indicates that people's subjective economic status is significantly affected by the objective relative income. Furthermore, results from both our OLS and IV estimates shows that the objective relative income has stronger impact on subjective economic status in area with stronger collectivist culture. Our paper sheds light on the important role of the institutional factor-culture plays on subjective welfare.

Social Network and Consumption Smoothing in Rural India (written with Songqing Jin, update: May 14, 2021).
Consumption smoothing, which measures the extent to which one can buffer shocks, is an important ability for rural farmers in developing countries where the formal credits or financial market are missing. A large body of literature has documented the crucial impact of social networks on consumption smoothing. Using a large-scale survey data from India, we investigate the consumption smoothing behavior in the context of rural India with a focus on caste-based social networks. Our result first indicates that while households’ consumption is partially insured, the full insurance or efficient risk-sharing within the village is rejected. Moreover, we instrument the idiosyncratic income change with rainfall deviation and find a smoother consumption within the backward caste groups. We also find that the consumption of households with intra-caste or intra-caste group interactions only is relatively smoother than households who also have interactions across castes or caste groups, which could be explained by the possible stronger intra-caste social interactions of the former group, and this provides indirect evidence of risk-sharing within the caste. Besides, our result suggests that households with experience of migration working do better in consumption smoothing. While this might be more likely a result of the extra buffer mechanism against idiosyncratic shock in agriculture, the potential stronger out-village social network could also contribute to it.
